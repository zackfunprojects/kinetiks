# Cold Email Outreach

Reference for writing cold emails that get replies. Covers first-touch structure, follow-up sequences, personalization depth, and reply optimization.

---

## First-Touch Structure

A cold email has five lines of work to do. Each line earns the right to the next.

**Line 1 — Personalized observation about them.**
Not flattery. A specific detail that proves you looked at their business, content, or situation. This is the permission to keep reading.
- "Saw your talk at [event] on [topic] — your point about X stuck with me."
- "Noticed [company] just launched [feature]. That usually means [challenge]."
- "Your post on [topic] mentioned [specific detail] — that's exactly the problem we solve."

**Lines 2-3 — The problem you solve, specific to their situation.**
Connect the observation to a pain point. Do not describe your product. Describe their problem.
- "Most teams in [their industry] waste 10+ hours/week on [task] because [reason]."
- "After a launch like that, the usual bottleneck is [specific challenge]."

**Line 4 — Credibility: one specific result or social proof.**
Not "we work with Fortune 500 companies." One concrete number or name.
- "We helped [similar company] cut that from 10 hours to 2."
- "[Recognizable customer] used this to [specific outcome]."

**Line 5 — Low-friction CTA: a question, not a demand.**
Never ask for a 30-minute call in a first email. Ask a question that is easy to answer.
- "Would it make sense to share how we did this?"
- "Is [problem] something your team is dealing with right now?"
- "Worth a quick look, or not the right time?"

## Follow-Up Escalation (5-Touch Sequence)

Most replies come from follow-ups, not the initial email. Each touch adds a new angle.

| Touch | Timing | Approach |
|-------|--------|----------|
| 1: Initial | Day 0 | First-touch structure above |
| 2: New Angle | +2 days | Different framing of the same problem. Add a relevant insight or data point. Do not just "bump" the previous email. |
| 3: Social Proof | +4 days | Share a specific customer result, case study, or testimonial relevant to their industry or role. |
| 4: Value-Add | +7 days | Send something genuinely useful with no ask: a relevant article, benchmark data, a tool recommendation. Generosity earns attention. |
| 5: Breakup | +14 days | Signal that this is the last email. "Looks like the timing isn't right — I'll stop reaching out. If [problem] comes up later, here's where to find me." Breakup emails often get the highest reply rate because they remove pressure. |

**Rules:**
- Every follow-up must add new information or a new angle. "Just following up" and "Bumping this to the top of your inbox" are delete-triggers.
- Reference the previous email briefly but do not rehash it.
- Keep the thread going (reply to your own email) so they see the history.

## Personalization Depth

Four levels, each increasing reply rate. Go as deep as time allows.

**Level 1 — Name + Company.**
Minimum viable personalization. Merge fields. Low effort, low impact. Better than nothing but not differentiated.

**Level 2 — Role + Industry.**
Reference their specific job function and industry challenges. Shows you understand their world, not just their name.
- "As a VP of Marketing at a Series B SaaS company, you're probably dealing with..."

**Level 3 — Trigger Event or Content.**
Reference something they recently published, launched, hired for, or experienced. This is where reply rates jump significantly.
- "Your blog post on [topic] mentioned [specific point] — we built a tool that addresses exactly that."
- "Saw you're hiring a [role] — that usually means [challenge] is top of mind."
- Sources: LinkedIn posts, company blog, job postings, funding announcements, product launches, conference talks.

**Level 4 — Mutual Connection or Shared Context.**
The highest-converting personalization. A warm introduction, shared experience, or mutual contact.
- "[Mutual contact] suggested I reach out — they thought [reason]."
- "We were both at [event] last month — your panel on [topic] resonated."
- If no mutual connection exists, shared context works: same investor, same accelerator, same industry group, same city.

## Reply Optimization

**Short emails win.** Under 100 words gets roughly 2x the reply rate of 200+ words. Every sentence must earn its place.

**One CTA per email.** Multiple asks create decision paralysis. One clear question.

**Question beats statement for CTA.**
- "Would it make sense to..." > "Let's schedule a call"
- "Is this a priority for your team right now?" > "I'd love to chat"
- "Worth a quick look?" > "Check out our demo"

**Tone:** Write like a peer, not a salesperson. No "I hope this email finds you well." No "I'd love to pick your brain." No "Just wanted to touch base."

**From line matters.** First name + last name outperforms company name. People reply to people.

**Send timing for cold email:**
- B2B: Tuesday-Thursday, 7-9 AM recipient's timezone
- Avoid Monday (inbox clearing) and Friday afternoon (mentally checked out)
- Second window: 1-2 PM (post-lunch scan)

**Subject lines for cold email:**
- Short (3-6 words), lowercase, no punctuation
- Reference them or their company, not you
- "quick question about [their initiative]"
- "[mutual contact] suggested I reach out"
- "[their company] + [your relevant topic]"
