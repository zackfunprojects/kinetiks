# Proof Escalation

Reference for selecting the right type and intensity of proof based on deal stage and objection type. Proof that is too weak fails to convince. Proof that is too strong too early feels desperate.

---

## Proof Hierarchy

Ordered from weakest to strongest. Each level carries more weight and requires more effort to produce.

**Level 1 — Claim.**
An unsupported assertion. "We're the fastest solution on the market." Carries almost no weight with skeptical buyers. Use only when introducing a topic for the first time to a new audience. Never rely on claims alone past the first touchpoint.

**Level 2 — Statistic.**
A quantified claim. "47% faster processing on average" or "Reduces onboarding time from 6 weeks to 4 days." More credible than a naked claim, but still your number. Prospects wonder: "Is that cherry-picked?" Best paired with a source or methodology note.

**Level 3 — Named example.**
A specific, identifiable customer and their result. "Acme Corp reduced processing time by 47%." The name makes it verifiable. The prospect can look up the company and confirm it is real. Significantly stronger than an anonymous stat.

**Level 4 — Case study.**
A detailed narrative of how a named customer achieved results. Includes the starting situation, the problem, the implementation, and the outcome with specific numbers. Case studies work because they let the prospect project themselves into the story: "Their situation sounds like ours."

**Level 5 — Reference call.**
A live conversation between the prospect and an existing customer. This is the most trusted form of social proof because the prospect controls the questions. They can ask about problems, not just wins. Offering a reference call signals confidence in your product.

**Level 6 — Free trial or proof of concept.**
The prospect experiences the value themselves. No intermediary, no narrative, no spin. They measure the results in their own environment. This is the strongest proof because it removes all reliance on your credibility — the product speaks for itself.

---

## When to Use Each Level

Match proof intensity to the deal stage. Escalating too fast wastes your best ammunition. Escalating too slow loses the deal.

| Stage | Appropriate Proof Levels | Rationale |
|-------|------------------------|-----------|
| Discovery | Level 1-2 | Do not overwhelm. The prospect is deciding whether to pay attention, not whether to buy. A strong stat is enough to earn a second conversation. |
| Evaluation | Level 3-4 | Prove it is real. The prospect is comparing you against alternatives and needs concrete evidence, not just claims. Named examples and case studies differentiate you from competitors who only have claims. |
| Decision | Level 4-5 | Remove all doubt. The prospect has chosen you but needs to convince others internally. Case studies and reference calls give the champion ammunition for the internal sell. |
| Close | Level 5-6 | Let them verify for themselves. At this stage, the prospect's remaining concerns are best addressed by hearing from a peer or testing the product directly. Nothing you say is as convincing as what they experience or hear from another buyer. |

---

## Proof Selection by Objection Type

Different objections require different types of proof, regardless of stage. Match the proof format to what the objection actually demands.

**Price objection** ("It's too expensive"):
Lead with ROI calculation (Level 2) — translate cost into return. Support with a customer savings story (Level 3-4) showing payback period. The goal is to reframe the conversation from cost to investment. Never respond to a price objection with features. Respond with financial outcomes.

**Feature objection** ("You don't have X" or "Competitor has Y"):
Lead with a demo or trial (Level 6) showing the actual workflow they need. Support with workaround documentation (Level 3) if the feature gap is real but solvable. If the feature genuinely does not exist and matters to them, be honest — forced positioning around a gap backfires.

**Trust objection** ("We've never heard of you" or "Are you stable enough?"):
Lead with named logos and customer count (Level 3). Support with a reference call (Level 5) from a customer they respect or recognize. Trust objections are about perceived risk, so stack multiple proof points — investor backing, team credentials, customer retention metrics, uptime guarantees.

**Timeline objection** ("Implementation takes too long" or "We need this by Q3"):
Lead with an implementation case study (Level 4) showing actual timelines from a comparable customer. Support with a pilot offer (Level 6) that gets them live on a subset quickly. Timeline objections often mask a deeper concern — fear of disruption — so address both the timeline and the change management approach.
