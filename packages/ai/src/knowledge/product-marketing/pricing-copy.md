# Pricing Copy

How to write pricing pages, tier descriptions, and value justification that converts.

---

## Tier Naming

Names should communicate who the tier is for, not just rank. Avoid generic "Basic / Pro / Enterprise" when possible.

**Good naming patterns:**
- "Starter > Growth > Scale" -- tells you the stage you are at
- "Solo > Team > Company" -- tells you the team size
- "Free > Pro > Business" -- acceptable when paired with clear descriptions

**Each tier name should answer:** "Is this for me?" at a glance. The name itself is the first filter.

---

## Feature Comparison Tables

**Lead with outcomes, not features.** Translate technical capabilities into user results:
- "Send 10,000 emails/month" > "Email module included"
- "Unlimited team members" > "Multi-user support"
- "Real-time analytics dashboard" > "Analytics"

**Highlight the recommended tier.** Most users should land on your mid-tier. Make it visually prominent (badge, border, "Most Popular" label). This is the anchor.

**Honest gaps:** If a lower tier does not include something, say so clearly. Hiding limitations erodes trust. Checkmarks and X-marks are clearer than asterisks and footnotes.

**Group features by category.** Core features, collaboration, integrations, support, security. Do not list 40 ungrouped features -- nobody reads that.

---

## Value Justification

**Anchor against the cost of not having the product.** Calculate what the prospect currently spends in time, money, or missed opportunity:
- "Teams spend 15 hours/week on manual outreach. At $50/hour, that's $3,000/month. This product is $199/month."
- "The average cost of [problem] is $X/year. Prevention costs $Y/month."

**ROI framing:** Express value as a multiplier. "For every $1 spent, teams report $X in saved time/increased revenue."

**Social proof on the pricing page:** Testimonials that reference value, not just satisfaction. "We saved 20 hours/week" is stronger than "Great product!"

**Annual vs monthly:** Show the annual discount prominently. Default the toggle to annual. Frame as "Save X%" not "Pay $X/year."

---

## Pricing Page FAQ

Address objections before they become blockers. The FAQ section on a pricing page is not informational -- it is objection handling.

**Must-answer questions:**
- "Can I switch plans later?" (reduces commitment anxiety)
- "What happens when I hit the limit?" (removes fear of surprise charges)
- "Is there a contract?" (if no, say so loudly)
- "Can I cancel anytime?" (reduce risk perception)
- "Do you offer a free trial?" (lower the barrier)
- "Is my data secure?" (enterprise concern)
- "Do you offer discounts for nonprofits/startups/education?" (if yes, say it here)

Each answer should be 1-3 sentences. Direct, no hedging. If the answer is favorable, lead with it boldly.

---

## CTA Per Tier

Each tier gets a CTA calibrated to its conversion path:

- **Free tier:** "Start free" or "Get started" -- zero friction language, no commitment implied
- **Mid tier (focus tier):** "Start free trial" or "Try free for 14 days" -- this is where most users should land, make the CTA the most prominent on the page
- **Top tier:** "Talk to sales" or "Book a demo" -- signals human interaction, appropriate for higher price points
- **Enterprise:** "Contact us" or "Get a custom quote" -- implies flexibility and customization

**Button design:** The focus tier CTA should be the most visually prominent (filled button, contrasting color). Other tiers use outlined or secondary button styles.

**Below-CTA reassurance:** "No credit card required" or "Cancel anytime" directly under the button. Removes the last micro-objection before click.
