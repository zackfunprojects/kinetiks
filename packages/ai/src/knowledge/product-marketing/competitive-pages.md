# Competitive Pages

Frameworks for comparison pages, migration guides, and category content that ranks and converts.

---

## Honest Comparison Framework

The most effective comparison format. Genuine fairness builds trust that converts.

**Page structure:**

1. **Hero:** "[Product] vs [Competitor]: An honest comparison." Subhead promises to show where each wins and help the reader choose.

2. **Quick summary table:** Feature-by-feature comparison with checkmarks. Be honest -- show features they have that you do not. Dishonesty here destroys credibility for every other claim on the page.

3. **Where we are better:** 3-5 advantages with evidence (metrics, screenshots, customer quotes). Each advantage maps to a specific competitor weakness.

4. **Where they are better:** 1-2 genuine advantages the competitor has. This section builds massive trust. Prospects who see honest competitor acknowledgment trust the rest of the page 3x more than those who read a page claiming superiority on everything.

5. **Who should choose us:** Specific use cases, team sizes, priorities where your product is the better fit.

6. **Who should choose them:** Be honest. If they serve a specific use case better, say so. You earn trust from every visitor who does not match that use case.

7. **Customer switch stories:** 2-3 people who switched from the competitor, with specific reasons and outcomes.

8. **CTA:** "Try [Product] free for 14 days. No credit card required."

**Why honesty wins:** Nobody believes a product is better at everything. Acknowledging competitor strengths makes every other claim on the page credible.

## Migration Guide

For products where switching costs create hesitation. Reduces anxiety by making the process concrete and supported.

**Page structure:**

1. **What migrates automatically:** Data, settings, integrations that transfer without effort.

2. **What needs manual setup:** Honest about the work required. Hiding this creates angry customers, not more customers.

3. **Timeline:** "Most teams complete migration in [X] hours/days." Specific and realistic.

4. **Migration support:** What your team does for them. Dedicated support, migration calls, data import assistance.

5. **What they gain:** Feature improvements they get after switching. This is the payoff section.

6. **What stays the same:** Reassurance that core workflows will not break. Familiarity reduces switching anxiety.

7. **CTA:** "Book a 15-minute migration walkthrough."

## Category Page

Target "[category] tools" and "best [category] software" searches. These are high-intent, research-phase queries.

**Page structure:**

1. **Hero:** "The best [category] tools in [year], compared."

2. **Quick ranking table:** 4-6 tools (including yours) scored on 5-6 criteria relevant to the buyer.

3. **Individual reviews:** 150-200 words per tool, genuinely fair. Your product gets slightly more depth (natural, not biased).

4. **Decision framework:** How to choose based on team size, budget, use case. This is the most valuable section for SEO and reader trust.

5. **Recommendation:** "If you are [ideal customer profile], we built [Product] for you."

6. **CTA:** "Start free."

**Critical rule:** Include 3-5 real alternatives. Pages that only push one product rank poorly and convert poorly. Google rewards comprehensive comparison content. Buyers trust it. Both incentives align.

## SEO Targeting

**Primary keywords:**
- "[Competitor] alternative"
- "[Competitor] vs [Your Product]"
- "best [Competitor] alternative"
- "switch from [Competitor]"

**Long-tail keywords:**
- "[Competitor] alternative for [use case]"
- "[Competitor] alternative for small teams"
- "cheaper alternative to [Competitor]"
- "[Competitor] alternative with [specific feature]"

These are purchase-intent keywords. Searchers are actively evaluating. Comparison pages convert at 3-5x the rate of generic landing pages.

## Competitive Intelligence (Battlecards)

### Four-Quadrant Framework

Organize intelligence into: their strengths, their weaknesses, our mapped advantages, and objection handling scripts.

**Their strengths (the honesty section):** For each strength, document what they do well, why it matters to the buyer, and how to acknowledge it. Pattern: acknowledge the strength, redirect to your advantage, ask a question that reveals the prospect's real priority.

**Their weaknesses (evidence-required):** Every weakness must have evidence.
- Tier 1 (strongest): review site ratings, customer churn data, public complaints
- Tier 2 (moderate): your own product testing, prospect feedback, analyst reports
- Tier 3 (directional): company culture signals, funding concerns, documentation gaps

**Our advantages (mapped to their weaknesses):** Do not list random advantages. Map each one directly to a competitor weakness. Prepare three versions: one-liner (quick objection handling), proof version (specific numbers), demo version (what to show live).

**Objection handling (ACE framework):**
- **A**cknowledge the concern -- do not dismiss it
- **C**ounter with your advantage, backed by proof
- **E**vidence with a specific customer story or metric

## Win/Loss Analysis

After every competitive deal, capture structured intelligence:

**Wins:** Primary reason they chose you (with quote), what almost lost the deal, what the competitor pitched, the key moment that tipped the decision.

**Losses:** Primary reason they chose the competitor (be brutally honest), what you did well, what you should have done differently, feature gap cited, price sensitivity, recovery potential.

**Aggregation:** After 10+ analyses, identify patterns: top 3 reasons you win, top 3 reasons you lose, win rate by competitor. Use patterns to update battlecards, inform product roadmap, and create targeted marketing content.
