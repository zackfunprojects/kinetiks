# Hooks

The hook is 80% of content performance. A great hook with mediocre content outperforms great content with a weak hook. Write 5 hooks for every piece and test them independently.

---

## Universal Hook Structures

**Contrarian statement:** Challenge a widely held belief. "Most people think X. They're wrong." Forces the reader to find out why.

**Specific number or result:** Concrete data creates instant credibility. "I analyzed 500 profiles" or "From $0 to $1M in 18 months" beats vague claims every time.

**Question that challenges an assumption:** Not rhetorical fluff -- a question that makes the reader realize they might be wrong. "Why do some founders grow fast while others stay stuck?"

**"Most people think X. They're wrong.":** The two-sentence structure that creates an irresistible curiosity gap. Setup the common belief, then deny it.

**Story opener with tension:** Start in the middle of the story, not the beginning. "I got fired in 2019. Best thing that ever happened." Tension before context.

**Personal failure to lesson:** Vulnerability + specificity. "I spent 10 years building the wrong career. Here's what I wish I knew at 25."

**Credibility + insight:** "After [impressive stat], I've noticed the top [role] all do this." Earned authority opens the door.

**Bold prediction:** "[Industry] is about to change forever. Here's what's coming." Stakes + forward-looking = engagement.

---

## Platform-Specific Hooks

**LinkedIn:** Personal story leading to business insight. Professional vulnerability works. "I [personal experience]. Here's what it taught me about [business topic]." Hooks that drove massive reach: failure stories, contrarian career advice, specific numbers from personal experience.

**Twitter/X:** Bold claim in under 280 characters. Must stand completely alone -- many readers never click into the thread. "The best marketing doesn't feel like marketing." Single-tweet hooks: sharp, declarative, no hedging.

**Instagram:** Visual arrest + text overlay. The image/first frame must stop the scroll before text is read. For carousels: slide 1 is bold text creating curiosity. For Reels: start mid-action, never with an intro.

**TikTok:** Pattern interrupt in first 2 seconds. Start mid-action or with a bold visual/verbal claim. Identity callouts ("This is for my [group]..."), proof-first (show the result immediately), or controversy spark ("I'm gonna get hate for this but..."). The hook is visual AND verbal simultaneously.

**YouTube:** Thumbnail + title ARE the hook. Expressive face + bold 3-4 word text overlay + curiosity gap. Title formulas: "How I [result] in [timeframe]", "[Number] mistakes [role] make", "Why [surprising thing] is [counterintuitive truth]".

---

## Hook Testing

- Write 5 hooks minimum for every piece of content
- Test hooks independently from the content body -- a hook's job is to earn the first paragraph, nothing more
- On platforms with edit capability, swap hooks on underperforming content
- Track which hook structures consistently perform for your audience
- The hook that feels most uncomfortable is often the best performer

---

## Anti-Patterns (Hooks That Kill Performance)

- "In today's fast-paced world..." -- generic, says nothing, screams AI-generated
- "I'm excited to share..." -- nobody cares about your excitement, lead with value
- "Great news!" -- corporate newsletter energy, instant scroll-past
- "We're thrilled to announce..." -- same problem, brand-centric not reader-centric
- Rhetorical questions nobody cares about: "Have you ever wondered about [obvious thing]?" -- if the answer is "not really," you lost
- Starting with context instead of tension: "Last week at a conference..." -- start with the insight, backfill the context
- Hedging language: "I think maybe possibly..." -- conviction hooks, uncertainty repels
- Clickbait without payoff: bold hook that the content doesn't deliver on destroys trust permanently
