# Voice Adaptation

Same voice, different register. A formal brand on Twitter is still formal -- but shorter and more direct. An informal brand writing a whitepaper is still informal -- but with more depth and structure.

---

## The Adaptation Principle

Voice adaptation is not voice changing. The underlying identity remains constant. What shifts is the register -- the level of formality, compression, and directness appropriate to the platform and format.

Think of it like a person speaking at a conference vs talking to a friend vs writing an email to a client. Same person, same personality, different mode.

---

## Platform Voice Shifts

**Email:** Most personal register. Direct address ("you"). Conversational tone. Shorter paragraphs. Personal anecdotes welcome. Feels like a message from a person, not a broadcast.

**Blog:** Most depth. Teaching mode. Longest form. Room for nuance, examples, and build-up. The voice can breathe -- longer sentences, more complex arguments, fuller explanations.

**Social media:** Most compressed. Hook-first. Every sentence earns the next. No warm-up, no preamble. The voice's personality must come through in fewer words.

**Landing page:** Most persuasive. Benefit-focused. Direct, clear, action-oriented. Less personality, more clarity. Every sentence moves toward the CTA.

**Press release:** Most neutral. Facts-first. Quotes carry the personality; the body carries the information. Least room for voice expression.

**Support/help docs:** Most utilitarian. Clear, scannable, task-oriented. Voice warmth shows through approachability and respect for the reader's time, not through personality.

**Newsletter:** Hybrid of email and blog. Personal opening, substantive middle, conversational close. The most "authentic self" register for most brands.

---

## What Never Changes

These are constants regardless of platform, format, or audience:

- **Vocabulary preferences:** The words the brand uses and avoids do not change. If "stakeholder" is banned, it is banned everywhere.
- **Personality traits:** If the voice is curious, it is curious in a tweet and in a whitepaper. The expression differs; the trait persists.
- **Things-we-never-say rules:** Banned phrases, emotional boundaries, framing rules. These are absolute constraints.
- **Core rhetorical patterns:** The characteristic way the brand builds arguments, uses tension, or lands points. Compressed on Twitter, expanded in a blog, but structurally recognizable.

---

## What Adapts

- **Sentence length:** Shorter on social, longer in blogs and whitepapers. The rhythm pattern scales but the variation signature remains.
- **Depth of explanation:** A social post states the insight. A blog post explains the insight. A whitepaper proves the insight. Same insight, different treatment.
- **CTA directness:** Social: "Follow for more." Email: "Hit reply." Landing page: "Start your free trial." Blog: "Read the next post in this series."
- **Humor frequency:** Social and newsletter can carry more humor. Landing pages and press releases carry less. Support docs carry almost none.
- **Formality level:** Within the brand's range on the formality spectrum, social sits at the casual end and press releases sit at the formal end. Neither should fall outside the brand's natural range.
