# Voice Profiling

A voice profile captures how a specific person, organization, or product communicates -- not just what words they use, but how they build momentum, transition between ideas, balance data with personality, and land a point.

---

## Three-Layer Voice System

Every piece of content uses up to three stacked voice layers. Lower layers constrain, upper layers personalize.

### Layer 1: User Voice (The Individual)

The individual's writing identity. Persists across every org and product because a person's natural voice does not fundamentally change when they switch contexts.

**Captures:**
- Sentence rhythm preferences (short punches after long explanations? compound sentences?)
- Vocabulary tendencies (philosophical references? technical precision? conversational contractions?)
- Transition style (thought bridges vs structural markers vs implicit flow)
- Rhetorical patterns (questions then answers? contrarian pivots? "here's the thing" turns?)
- Warmth integration (woven through information, or reserved for openings/closings?)
- Structural preferences (where they place the strongest point, how they organize arguments)

### Layer 2: Org Voice (The Brand)

The organization's communication identity. Acts as a constraint layer -- defines what is and is not allowed regardless of who is writing.

**Captures:**
- Voice adjectives (e.g., warm, curious, specific, honest, actionable)
- Banned phrases and patterns (with reasons for each ban)
- Required patterns (active voice, person-first language, etc.)
- Tone calibration by content type (blog vs guide vs landing page)
- Emotional boundaries (what emotions the brand never uses)
- Subject-matter framing rules

### Layer 3: Product Voice (The Subject)

Optional. Adds product-specific language rules when content is about a particular product.

**Captures:**
- Product terminology (approved names, feature language)
- Metaphor library (approved metaphors and analogies)
- Competitor framing (how to reference alternatives)
- Technical accuracy requirements
- Integration language (how the product is introduced in content)

### How Layers Stack

Apply from bottom up: content structure first, then product voice constraints, then org voice constraints, then user voice characteristics. **Conflicts resolve downward:** if the user's natural style uses a pattern the org bans, the org rule wins. The org voice is the guardrail; the user voice is the personality within those guardrails.

## Tone Spectrum Analysis

Map the brand across these dimensions. Each is a 1-10 scale with descriptions at both poles.

| Dimension | 1 (Low) | 10 (High) |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| Formality | Casual, contractions, fragments, slang | Formal, complete sentences, no contractions |
| Energy | Calm, measured, understated | High-energy, exclamation points, urgency |
| Humor | Serious, no jokes, gravity | Frequent humor, wordplay, lightness |
| Warmth | Direct, no-nonsense, clinical | Personal, empathetic, caring |
| Technical depth | Plain language, no jargon | Domain-specific, assumes expertise |
| Boldness | Hedged, careful, diplomatic | Strong claims, no qualifiers, conviction |

Record the brand's position on each dimension with evidence from actual content.

## Vocabulary Extraction

**Preferred terms:** Words the brand actively uses. Include preferred connectors (e.g., "but" and "and" over "furthermore" and "additionally"). Note contraction usage (always/never/sometimes) and jargon tolerance level.

**Avoided terms:** Words the brand never uses, each with a reason. Generic superlatives ("incredible," "amazing"), corporate filler ("stakeholder," "leverage"), AI-sounding connectors ("moreover," "it is worth noting that").

**Signature phrases:** Recurring expressions that make the brand recognizable. Transition phrases, sentence starters, characteristic turns of phrase.

---

## Rhythm Detection

**Sentence length patterns:** Average length, variation range, whether the writer uses short punches (3-8 words) after long explanations (25-35 words). Maximum consecutive sentences of similar length before it sounds flat.

**Transition style:** Thought bridges (connecting the last idea to the next through shared concepts) vs structural markers ("Furthermore," "Additionally") vs implicit flow (letting ideas connect without explicit markers). The best writing uses thought bridges.

**Rhetorical patterns:** Questions then answers? Contrarian pivots? Short declarative statements for emphasis? Lists or flowing prose? Parenthetical asides? Sentence fragments for effect?

**Paragraph structure:** Length preferences. Whether paragraphs end on strong points or transition phrases. How the writer opens paragraphs (topic sentence? continuation? question?).

---

## Extraction Sources

Priority order for extracting voice profiles:

1. **Founder/author's personal writing** -- most authentic signal. Emails, social posts, blog posts they wrote themselves.
2. **Website copy** -- homepage, about page, key landing pages. Strip navigation and boilerplate.
3. **Social posts** -- especially high-performing ones (the audience validated these as on-brand).
4. **Emails and newsletters** -- more personal register, reveals natural voice.
5. **Support docs and help content** -- shows how the brand explains things.
6. **Video/podcast transcripts** -- spoken word reveals natural rhythm and vocabulary.

**Extraction method:** Analyze samples for patterns across all dimensions above. Use specific quoted examples as evidence for each finding. Cross-reference findings across multiple sources to distinguish consistent voice traits from one-off variations.

**What the user does vs what they say:** Analyzing actual writing samples is more reliable than asking someone to describe their voice. People describe aspirational voice; their writing reveals actual voice.
