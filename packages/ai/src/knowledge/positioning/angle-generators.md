# Angle Generators

Eight proven frameworks for finding positioning angles that differentiate and convert. Each generator includes the pattern, when to use it, and an example.

---

## 1. Contrarian

Challenge an accepted industry belief. Position against conventional wisdom.

**Pattern:** "Everyone says X. Here's why that's wrong."

**When to use:** When the market follows a dominant practice that has real downsides no one discusses. Works best at market sophistication Stage 3-5 where audiences are tired of the same claims.

**Example:** "Everyone says you need a 10-page business plan. We say you need a one-page canvas and 30 days of customer conversations."

**Key:** The contrarian claim must be defensible. Back it with evidence or a logical argument. Contrarian for shock value without substance backfires.

---

## 2. Unique Mechanism

Name the proprietary method, system, or process that powers your solution. Gives the audience a reason to believe this approach is different.

**Pattern:** "The [Named Method/System] that [outcome]."

**When to use:** Market sophistication Stage 3-4. When audiences have heard the promise before but need to understand why your approach delivers where others did not.

**Example:** "The 8-Layer Context Structure that gives AI your complete brand voice — not just a tone selector."

**Key:** The mechanism must be specific and nameable. Vague descriptions ("our proprietary technology") do not work. Explain what it does, not just that it exists.

---

## 3. Transformation

Frame the product as a before/after journey. Focus on the identity shift or state change, not features.

**Pattern:** "From [painful current state] to [desired end state]."

**When to use:** When the outcome is vivid and emotionally resonant. Works across all sophistication stages because transformation is universally compelling.

**Example:** "From scattered data across 12 tools to unified intelligence in one dashboard."

**Key:** Make both states concrete and specific. "From struggling to successful" is weak. "From 3 hours of manual reporting every Monday to a live dashboard that updates itself" is strong.

---

## 4. Enemy

Name what you are against. Create an us-vs-them dynamic with a practice, system, or status quo — not a competitor by name.

**Pattern:** "We built this because we hate [the enemy]."

**When to use:** When your audience shares a common frustration with an existing approach, tool category, or industry practice. Creates tribal identity.

**Example:** "We built this because we hate disconnected marketing tools that force you to copy-paste between six dashboards."

**Key:** The enemy should be a practice or problem, not a specific competing company. Attacking competitors by name looks petty. Attacking a broken system looks principled.

---

## 5. Speed/Ease

Reduce the perceived time and effort required to achieve the outcome. Directly attacks the denominator of the value equation.

**Pattern:** "[Outcome], [dramatically reduced time/effort]."

**When to use:** When competitors' solutions are slow, complex, or require significant setup. Particularly effective for technical products where the status quo involves painful implementation.

**Example:** "One identity, every app, set up in 10 minutes. Not 10 weeks."

**Key:** The speed/ease claim must be specific and verifiable. "Fast" is meaningless. "Set up in 10 minutes" is testable. Pair with a comparison to the current painful alternative.

---

## 6. Specificity

Use extreme precision to build credibility. Specific numbers, exact results, and precise details signal real experience over marketing fluff.

**Pattern:** "[Exact metric] across [exact scope]."

**When to use:** When you have real data. Works at any sophistication stage because specificity is universally more believable than generalities.

**Example:** "47% faster pipeline velocity across 200+ enterprise accounts in the last 12 months."

**Key:** Every number must be accurate and defensible. Round numbers ("50% improvement") feel like estimates. Odd numbers ("47% improvement") feel like measurements. Source the data when possible.

---

## 7. Social Proof

Let others make the claim for you. Third-party validation is more credible than self-promotion.

**Pattern:** "[Specific proof point] — don't take our word for it."

**When to use:** When you have strong customer results, recognizable logos, or impressive adoption numbers. Works at sophistication Stage 2+ where audiences need proof beyond promises.

**Example:** "Used by 500+ marketing teams including [recognizable company], [recognizable company], and [recognizable company]."

**Key:** Specificity applies here too. "Trusted by thousands" is weaker than "Used by 2,847 teams." Named customers are stronger than anonymous counts. Results ("helped [company] grow revenue 3x") are stronger than logos alone.

---

## 8. Risk Reversal

Remove the downside of trying. Eliminate every barrier between interest and action.

**Pattern:** "[Low/no risk entry point]. [Explicit removal of common fears]."

**When to use:** When the audience has been burned before or when switching costs create hesitation. Especially effective for new market entrants competing against established players.

**Example:** "Free for 14 days. No credit card required. Cancel anytime. Export all your data if you leave."

**Key:** Address the specific fears your audience has. "Money-back guarantee" is generic. "If you don't see a 20% improvement in 60 days, we'll refund every penny and you keep the templates" is targeted risk reversal.

---

## Combining Angles

The strongest positioning often combines 2-3 generators:
- Contrarian + Unique Mechanism: "Everyone says X. Our [Named Method] proves why Y works better."
- Transformation + Specificity: "From [state A] to [state B] — with a 47% improvement in [metric]."
- Enemy + Risk Reversal: "We hate [broken system] so much we'll let you try the alternative free for 30 days."

Start with the generator that maps to your strongest differentiation, then layer in supporting angles.

---

## The Angle Discovery Process

Angles are discovered through research, not creative brainstorming.

1. **Research exhaustively** — document every fact about the product, the market, and competitive advertising
2. **Understand the prospect** — what do they already want, what keeps them awake, what have they tried before
3. **Assess market sophistication** — what stage is this market at, what type of angle will cut through
4. **Find the differentiation** — what is unique, what ordinary facts have not been highlighted, what can you claim that competitors cannot
5. **Structure for persuasion** — Problem > Promise > Proof > Proposition
6. **Test and measure** — do not guess; let results guide refinement
