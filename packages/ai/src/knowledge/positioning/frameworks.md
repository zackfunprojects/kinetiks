# Positioning Frameworks

Reference for product positioning, offer architecture, and market sophistication analysis.

---

## Obviously Awesome — Dunford's 5-Step Positioning Process

Positioning is context-setting, not feature-listing. It is the opening scene that tells the audience: where are we, what is happening, and how should I feel about it. Products do not have inherent positioning — positioning is a choice about how to frame the product in the customer's mind.

### The 5 Components (in order)

**Step 1: Competitive Alternatives**
What would customers do if your product did not exist? This is broader than "competitors":
- Do nothing (live with the problem)
- Use a spreadsheet or manual process
- Hire someone (agency, freelancer, consultant)
- Build it themselves
- Use a completely different type of solution
- Use a direct competitor

Rank alternatives by how often customers actually consider each one. Your differentiation only exists relative to these alternatives.

**Step 2: Unique Attributes**
What do you have that alternatives do not? List everything — features, approach, methodology, business model, team expertise, technology, partnerships, integrations. Do not judge importance yet. These are raw materials for differentiation.

**Step 3: Value (the "So What?" translation)**
What value do those unique attributes enable for customers? Features are not value. Translate each attribute:
- "[Unique attribute] enables [customer value]"
- Keep asking "so what?" until you hit something financial or emotional
- Chain: Feature > Functional benefit > Business impact > Emotional payoff

**Step 4: Target Customer Segments**
Who cares a lot about the value you deliver? Not everyone values your differentiation equally. Best-fit customers:
- Have the problem acutely
- Value your specific differentiation
- Have budget and authority to buy
- Can be reached through accessible channels

The narrower the segment, the stronger the positioning. "Shopify store owners doing $1M-$10M who struggle with inventory forecasting" beats "small businesses."

**Step 5: Market Category**
What market frame makes your value obvious? The category sets expectations. Calling yourself a "CRM" triggers different assumptions than "Sales Intelligence Platform."

Category options:
- Existing category (compete within established space)
- Adjacent category (borrow from a related space)
- New category (create your own space)

The right category makes your unique value obvious, puts you in favorable competitive context, and aligns with how customers think about the problem.

### Key Insight
Change one component and you affect all five. Positioning is a system, not a list of independent decisions.

### Common Mistakes
1. Positioning to everyone — if everyone is your customer, no one is
2. Leading with features — features are not value; translate them
3. Copying competitors — you cannot out-position someone in their own positioning
4. Ignoring alternatives — without knowing what you are compared against, you cannot differentiate
5. Wrong category — a category that makes your differentiation invisible kills you

### When to Revisit
- Product capabilities have significantly changed
- Target customer has evolved
- Competitive landscape has shifted
- Current positioning is not resonating (pipeline or win-rate problems)

## Offer Architecture — The Value Equation

**Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood) / (Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice)**

### Maximizing the Numerator

**Dream Outcome:** The transformation, not the process. The end state, not the tool.
- Not "fitness coaching" but "lose 20 lbs and keep it off"
- Not "marketing help" but "$100K in new revenue"
- Not "course access" but "launch your business in 30 days"

**Perceived Likelihood:** The buyer's belief they will actually achieve the outcome.
- Increase with: testimonials, case studies, guarantees, credibility, specificity, track record
- Specificity is key — "47% faster pipeline velocity across 200+ accounts" beats "improves performance"

### Minimizing the Denominator

**Time Delay:** How long until they see results?
- Quick wins early build momentum
- Faster time-to-value = higher perceived value
- Same outcome delivered faster commands premium pricing

**Effort & Sacrifice:** How much work do they have to do?
- Done-for-you beats done-with-you beats do-it-yourself
- Templates and systems reduce effort
- Every friction point removed increases value

If time delay or effort approaches zero, perceived value approaches infinity. This is why "instant" and "effortless" solutions command premium prices.

### Building the Offer
1. Define the dream outcome with specificity
2. List every obstacle between current state and dream outcome (external and internal)
3. Transform each obstacle into a solution
4. Choose delivery vehicles (done-for-you / done-with-you / do-it-yourself; 1-on-1 / group / 1-to-many)
5. Trim low-value elements, stack high-value ones

### Guarantee Types
- **Unconditional:** Full refund, no questions. Highest risk reversal.
- **Conditional:** Refund if specific conditions met (completed the program, implemented the steps).
- **Outcome-based:** Payment tied to results.
- Stack multiple guarantees for overwhelming risk reversal.

### Pricing Principle
Increase perceived value rather than decreasing price. If they perceive $50,000 of value and you charge $5,000, the decision is easy. Price-to-value discrepancy of at least 10:1 is the target.

## Market Sophistication — Schwartz's 5 Stages

Determines what type of messaging will work based on how much the market has already heard.

| Stage | Market State | Messaging Approach |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Market has never seen a product like this | State the promise directly. Simple, bold claim. "Lose weight while you sleep." |
| 2 | Competing claims exist but are not yet exhausted | Enlarge and quantify the promise. Bigger numbers, faster timelines. "Lose 20 lbs in 30 days." |
| 3 | Market is skeptical of promises | Introduce a unique mechanism — the HOW behind the claim. "The ketone activation method that burns fat 24/7." |
| 4 | Even mechanisms are questioned | Prove and enlarge the mechanism. More evidence, bigger proof, more specificity. |
| 5 | Market is exhausted and cynical | Appeal to identity, not outcomes. "For the founder who has tried everything." Or use a completely novel angle unrelated to existing claims. |

### Assessing the Stage
- Stage 1-2: Few competitors, audiences respond to direct claims
- Stage 3: Multiple competitors making similar promises, audience needs to understand why yours is different
- Stage 4-5: Crowded market, audience has been marketed to extensively, requires unique mechanism or identity play

Most B2B SaaS markets are at Stage 3-4. Most consumer wellness markets are at Stage 4-5.

## Positioning Statement Template

**For [target customer] who [situation/need], [product] is the [market category] that [key benefit/differentiation]. Unlike [primary alternative], we [unique differentiator].**

Each element maps to Dunford's 5 steps:
- [target customer] = Step 4
- [situation/need] = the problem implied by Step 1
- [market category] = Step 5
- [key benefit] = Step 3
- [primary alternative] = Step 1
- [unique differentiator] = Step 2 translated through Step 3

### Market Selection Criteria
Before positioning, verify the market has:
1. **Massive pain** — genuine suffering, financial loss, or urgent problem (not mild inconvenience)
2. **Purchasing power** — budget and willingness to spend on solutions
3. **Reachability** — identifiable channels to find and engage them
