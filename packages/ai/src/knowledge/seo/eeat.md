# E-E-A-T Signals

Google evaluates content on Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness. These signals must be woven naturally into the content -- not bolted on as a checklist.

## Experience Signals

First-person evidence that the author has directly done, used, or tested what they write about.

**What works:**
- "I tested this on 50 sites" -- actual implementation, not theoretical advice
- Specific numbers from real work: "organic traffic went from 12K to 89K monthly in four months"
- Naming mistakes made: "I ignored email for two years. Then I looked at the numbers."
- Personal narrative arc: struggled, discovered, succeeded
- Time investment visible: "I spent 6 months researching this"
- Methodology transparency: "Here's how I tested it, and what the results were"

**What fails:**
- "Studies show..." with no indication the author engaged with the studies
- Generic examples: "a business might..." instead of a real story
- Aggregation without application -- listing what others say without adding original insight

**The test:** Could this content only have been written by someone who actually did the thing?

---

## Expertise Signals

Technical depth and original thinking that demonstrates genuine knowledge of the subject.

**What works:**
- Original data or research others cannot replicate by aggregating existing sources
- Frameworks the author developed from experience (not borrowed without attribution)
- Going deeper than surface-level: explaining WHY something works, not just WHAT to do
- Debunking common misconceptions with evidence: "Most habit advice says 21 days. That number comes from a 1960s plastic surgeon. It has nothing to do with behavioral habits."
- Nuanced positions: not "X good" or "X bad" but reasoned analysis with acknowledged tradeoffs

**What fails:**
- Surface-level coverage that restates what the top 5 Google results already say
- Balanced-to-the-point-of-useless: "Some say X, others say Y" without committing to a position
- Jargon used to signal expertise without actually explaining anything

---

## Authoritativeness Signals

Evidence that the author and publication are recognized sources on this topic.

**What works:**
- Author credentials stated naturally: "After 10 years building RevOps teams at Fortune 500s..."
- Citations of primary sources with specifics: researcher names, sample sizes, publication details
- Expert quotes from named individuals at named organizations
- Cross-references from other authoritative sources linking to this content
- Track record: "We analyzed 2.7 million cold emails" or "I've reviewed 10,000+ job posts"

**What fails:**
- Anonymous authority: "experts say" or "research shows" without naming anyone
- Self-declared authority without evidence
- Credentials that do not relate to the topic at hand

---

## Trustworthiness Signals

Transparency, honesty, and accuracy that make readers (and search engines) confident in the content.

**What works:**
- Source attribution: link to studies, name the researchers, provide dates
- Admitted limitations: "This won't work for everyone. If you're in YMYL niches, different rules apply."
- Admitted uncertainty: "I'm not 100% sure why this works. But I've seen it across 40+ sites."
- Recency: content with current dates, updated statistics, references to recent events
- Distinguishing fact from opinion: "The data shows X. My interpretation is Y."
- Showing failed approaches alongside successes: "Our first approach didn't work. Here's what we learned."

**What fails:**
- Making claims without supporting evidence
- Never admitting any limitation, uncertainty, or mistake
- Outdated information presented as current
- Hiding affiliations, sponsorships, or conflicts of interest

---

## Weaving E-E-A-T Naturally

E-E-A-T signals should emerge from how the content is written, not from bolted-on sections.

**Instead of a credentials box:** Open with a story that demonstrates experience. "When I implemented this for an e-commerce brand, their organic traffic went from 12K to 89K in four months."

**Instead of a generic disclaimer:** Admit specific limitations inline. "This approach works for B2B SaaS. If you're in e-commerce or local services, the dynamics are different."

**Instead of citing studies in a bibliography:** Integrate evidence into the argument. "Researchers at Oxford tracked 248 people. After 66 days -- not 21 -- a clear habit pattern emerged."

**Instead of listing credentials:** Let the depth of the content prove expertise. An author who explains the nuance of relative vs. absolute risk demonstrates medical expertise more convincingly than a title.

**The ultimate test:** Compare the content to the best human-written examples in the niche. Does it have a specific viewpoint, real experience showing through, opinions defended with reasoning, unique insight, and a recognizable voice? If not, it will read as AI-generated regardless of technical optimization.
