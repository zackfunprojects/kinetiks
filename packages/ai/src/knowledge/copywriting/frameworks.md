# Copywriting Frameworks

## Awareness Levels (Schwartz)

Every audience sits at one of five awareness levels. The headline approach and copy length must match the level. This is the foundational concept -- get it wrong and nothing else matters.

**Level 1 -- Unaware:** The reader does not know they have a problem.
Approach: Lead with identity, emotion, or story. Never mention the product.
Headline: "The hidden cost of manual outreach that nobody talks about"

**Level 2 -- Problem-Aware:** They know the problem but not that solutions exist.
Approach: Name the problem vividly, agitate it, then introduce the solution category.
Headline: "Are you ever tongue-tied at a party?"

**Level 3 -- Solution-Aware:** They know solutions exist but not your product.
Approach: Lead with your unique mechanism or differentiator.
Headline: "A new way to remove wrinkles without surgery"

**Level 4 -- Product-Aware:** They know your product but haven't bought.
Approach: Overcome objections, stack proof, create urgency.
Headline: "Join 10,000 happy customers" or "Now with 2x the cleaning power"

**Level 5 -- Most Aware:** They want it. Just need a push.
Approach: Lead with the deal. Price, offer, availability.
Headline: "$199 today only" or "Back in stock"

**The length rule:** Less aware = longer copy (more education needed). Most aware = short and direct.

## AIDA Framework

**Attention:** Interrupt the pattern. Headlines, hooks, bold claims. Stop the scroll.
**Interest:** Build relevance. Show you understand their world. Present the problem or opportunity.
**Desire:** Create wanting. Paint the transformation. Stack proof and benefits.
**Action:** Tell them exactly what to do next. One clear CTA.

---

## PAS Framework

**Problem:** State the problem they recognize. Be specific -- use their language.
**Agitation:** Twist the knife. Show consequences of inaction. Make the pain vivid and urgent.
**Solution:** Present your offering as the resolution. Bridge from pain to relief.

PAS works because people are motivated more by avoiding pain than gaining pleasure. The agitation step is where most copy is too timid.

---

## Before-After-Bridge

**Before:** Paint their current painful reality. Be specific enough that they think "that's me."
**After:** Show the transformed state. What does life look like when the problem is solved?
**Bridge:** Your product or service is the bridge between the two states.

Modern shorthand version: "Ship your startup in days, not weeks." Before and after in six words, product implied as the bridge.

## Proof Patterns

**Specificity (Hopkins):** Specific claims create believability. "$1,237" beats "over $1,000." "99.6% of bacteria" beats "almost all bacteria." Details signal honesty -- liars round up.

**Borrowed Credibility (Ogilvy):** Use facts, quotes, or associations from trusted sources. Ogilvy's Rolls-Royce headline came from a magazine review. The credibility was borrowed, not manufactured.

**The Slippery Slide (Sugarman):** Every element has one job -- get them to read the next element. Headline pulls to subheadline. Subheadline pulls to first sentence. First sentence pulls to second. Each paragraph slides them toward the CTA.

**Conversation Entry (Collier):** Enter the conversation already happening in the reader's mind. You do not create desire. You channel existing desire toward your product.

**Reason-Why (Hopkins):** Do not just claim. Explain WHY your product works. Hopkins researched Schlitz beer for 3 weeks to find their purification process. Every brewery did it -- only Schlitz talked about it.

## Bucket Brigades

Transition phrases that maintain reading momentum between paragraphs:

- Here's the thing:
- But that's not even the best part.
- Now here's where it gets interesting.
- The result?
- Think about it.
- Turns out...
- Truth is...
- Bottom line:
- What does this mean?
- Which brings me to...

Place at paragraph endings or section transitions. Use 2-4 per page -- more becomes tiresome.

**Seeds of curiosity** (Sugarman's variant): End paragraphs with hooks that pull into the next section.
- "But there's a problem."
- "What happened next surprised even me."
- "Let me explain why."

## AI-Tell Detection

Patterns that flag copy as AI-generated. Avoid all of these.

**Word-level tells:**
- delve, comprehensive, robust, cutting-edge
- utilize (say "use"), leverage (as verb)
- crucial, vital, essential, pivotal
- unlock, unleash, supercharge, game-changer
- landscape, navigate, streamline, tapestry
- foster, facilitate, enhance, realm, paradigm
- plethora, myriad, nuanced, seamless

**Phrase-level tells:**
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "It's important to note that..."
- "When it comes to..."
- "Whether you're a... or a..."
- "Let's dive in" / "Let's explore"
- "This comprehensive guide will..."
- "In order to..." (just say "to")

**Structure-level tells:**
- Everything in threes (three benefits, three examples, three subpoints)
- Perfect parallelism (every bullet identical length and structure)
- Hedge stacking ("While X, it's important to consider Y, but also Z")
- Fake objectivity ("Some experts say... others believe...")
- Summary sandwich (intro summarizes, body covers, conclusion re-summarizes)

**Voice-level tells:**
- No opinions anywhere -- everything balanced, nothing claimed
- No mistakes or limitations mentioned
- Generic examples ("a business might...") instead of real stories
- Uniform certainty -- every statement equally confident
- Writing about a topic rather than from experience of it

**Fixes:**
- Vary sentence length aggressively. Short punch. Then longer explanatory sentences.
- Use fragments. For emphasis.
- Include parenthetical asides (like you'd say out loud to a friend).
- State opinions with reasoning. Real experts have takes.
- Admit limitations and uncertainty where honest.
- Add specific numbers from real experience.
- Read aloud -- if you stumble, readers will too.
