# Touchpoint Design

Reference for calibrating the number, mix, and pacing of campaign touchpoints based on deal complexity, audience type, and engagement signals.

---

## Touchpoint Budget by Deal Complexity

The number of touchpoints required to close a deal scales with price, risk, and the number of decision-makers involved. More touchpoints does not mean more emails — it means more diverse interactions across more channels.

**Simple product ($0-$500, single decision-maker, low risk):**
3-5 touchpoints total. One or two channels. The buyer can evaluate and decide quickly. Over-nurturing a simple purchase creates friction, not trust. Example: ad → landing page → follow-up email → conversion.

**Mid-market ($500-$25K, small buying group, moderate risk):**
7-12 touchpoints total. Three or four channels. The buyer needs to evaluate, compare, and possibly get internal approval. Content and email carry the heaviest load, with social reinforcement. Example: content discovery → email nurture (3-4 touches) → demo → proposal → follow-up → close.

**Enterprise ($25K+, large buying committee, high risk):**
15-25 touchpoints total. Five or more channels. Multiple stakeholders need different information. The champion needs ammunition to sell internally. Expect the cycle to involve content, email, outreach, events, reference calls, and custom collateral. No single channel closes an enterprise deal.

**The diversification rule:** If more than 60% of your touchpoints are on a single channel, rebalance. An 8-touch campaign where 7 touches are email is not a multi-channel campaign — it is an email campaign that will hit fatigue walls.

## Channel Mix by Audience Type

Different audiences live on different channels and respond to different proof types. Match the mix to the buyer.

**Developer audience:**
Content-heavy: documentation, tutorials, technical blog posts, open source contributions. Community channels: Reddit, Hacker News, Discord, Stack Overflow. Email should be minimal and high-signal — developers unsubscribe fast from anything that feels like marketing. Never lead with sales messaging. Lead with utility.

**Marketing audience:**
Social-heavy: LinkedIn and Twitter are primary discovery channels. Email-heavy: marketers respect good email because they write it themselves. Content focus: case studies with real numbers, tactical guides, benchmarks. This audience appreciates polished creative and strong copywriting.

**Executive audience:**
PR and thought leadership: executives read industry publications, not blog posts. Email must be extremely concise and high-signal — one paragraph maximum. Outreach works only if highly personalized and referencing a mutual connection or specific business trigger. Never send an executive a "nurture sequence." Send them one thing worth their time.

**SMB audience:**
Product-led: free trial or freemium is the primary acquisition channel. Email nurture focused on activation and quick wins. Social proof matters more than thought leadership — SMB buyers want to see that businesses like theirs succeeded. Price sensitivity is high, so ROI messaging must be concrete and fast.

## Touchpoint Fatigue Rules

Fatigue is the silent killer of campaigns. It does not announce itself — engagement just gradually drops until the channel is burned.

**Frequency caps:**
- No more than 2 email touches per week to the same contact (excluding transactional)
- No more than 1 cold outreach touch per week
- Social posting can be daily, but direct engagement (DMs, comments on their posts) should be no more than 2-3 times per week

**Fatigue signals and responses:**
- Email open rate drops below 15% → reduce frequency by half before changing content. Fatigue is more often a frequency problem than a content problem.
- Email unsubscribe rate exceeds 0.5% per send → immediately reduce frequency and audit content relevance.
- Social engagement drops for 3 consecutive posts → change the angle or format, not the frequency. Posting less does not fix weak content.
- Cold outreach reply rate drops below 2% → stop the sequence and rewrite. Do not increase volume to compensate for poor messaging.

## Re-engagement Triggers

When a contact goes silent, switching channels is more effective than increasing volume on the same channel.

**14 days no activity** → Switch channel. If email has gone silent, try a LinkedIn touchpoint or a content share. A new channel resets attention.

**30 days no activity** → Reduce to monthly digest or low-frequency check-in. The prospect is not ready. Stay visible without being intrusive. One valuable touch per month keeps the door open.

**60 days no activity** → Send a re-engagement email with a direct question: "Still exploring solutions for [problem]?" or "Should I keep you on our updates list?" A yes/no question forces a decision and cleans your list.

**90 days no activity** → Sunset sequence. One final email acknowledging the silence, offering to remove them, and leaving the door open. "No hard feelings — if this becomes a priority again, here is where to find us." Move non-responders to a cold list. Do not continue spending active campaign resources on them.
