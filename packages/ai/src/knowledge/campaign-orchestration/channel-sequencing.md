# Multi-Channel Campaign Orchestration

Reference for connecting individual channel tactics into coherent cross-channel campaigns. Covers channel roles, sequential flows, message progression, and timing coordination.

---

## Channel Role Framework

Each channel has a primary job in the buyer journey. Using a channel outside its primary role wastes budget and confuses the prospect.

**Discovery channels** (they find you):
- Search/SEO content: Captures existing demand. The prospect has a question or problem and finds your content. This is the highest-intent inbound channel.

**Awareness channels** (they see you repeatedly):
- Social media: Builds familiarity and trust through repeated exposure. Not a conversion channel — a warming channel. The goal is recognition, not clicks.
- PR/media: Third-party validation at scale. Others talk about you, which carries more weight than you talking about yourself. Best for credibility and reach.

**Nurture channels** (you talk to them directly):
- Email: The only channel you own completely. No algorithm decides who sees your message. Primary job is moving known contacts from interest to action.

**Activation channels** (you reach them directly):
- Cold outreach: Targets specific people who have not opted in. High precision, low volume. Works when you know exactly who needs your solution.

**Conversion channels** (they decide):
- Landing pages: The decision point. Every other channel ultimately drives here. One page, one offer, one action.

**Acceleration channels** (speed up any of the above):
- Paid ads: Not a standalone strategy. Paid amplifies whatever is already working. It makes a good funnel faster — it does not fix a broken one.

## Sequential Channel Maps

Campaigns fail when channels operate independently. These maps show which channel feeds which.

### Inbound Flow
SEO content (prospect discovers an article) → email capture (content upgrade or newsletter opt-in on the page) → nurture sequence (3-5 emails deepening the topic) → landing page (specific offer tied to the pain from the original content) → conversion.

The key: the email sequence must bridge the topic of the content they found to the product you sell. Do not jump from "helpful article" to "buy now." The sequence earns the right to pitch.

### Outbound Flow
Cold email (personalized first touch) → LinkedIn connection request within 24 hours (second channel, lower pressure) → content share on day 3 (value-add, not a pitch) → email follow-up sequence (new angles, social proof) → demo or call.

The key: LinkedIn and content touches make follow-up emails feel less cold. The prospect has now seen your name in three places, which triggers familiarity bias.

### PR Flow
Press release or media pitch → media coverage (earned placement) → social amplification (share and comment on the coverage) → content repurpose (turn the coverage into a blog post, quote graphic, or case study) → email to list ("As featured in [publication]...").

The key: PR has a short shelf life. You must have a distribution plan ready before the coverage lands. A press hit with no amplification plan reaches a fraction of its potential audience.

### Product-Led Flow
Free trial or freemium signup → onboarding email sequence (activate the user in the first 48 hours) → feature discovery (in-app prompts + email tips showing unused features) → upgrade prompt (triggered by usage milestones or feature gates) → expansion (upsell existing customers to higher tiers).

The key: the onboarding sequence is the campaign. If users do not reach the activation milestone (the moment they experience core value), nothing downstream converts.

## Message Progression Across Channels

Each touchpoint must advance the conversation. If the prospect reads all your touches in sequence, it should feel like a developing argument, not a broken record.

**Touch 1 — Identify the problem** (content or ad):
Name the pain specifically. Do not mention your solution. The goal is head-nodding: "Yes, that is exactly my problem." Use data, anecdotes, or provocative observations.

**Touch 2 — Demonstrate deep understanding** (social or email):
Go deeper on the problem. Show you understand the nuances, the failed attempts, the downstream consequences. This is where trust forms — they believe you understand their world.

**Touch 3 — Introduce the solution framework** (email or content):
Present the approach, not the product. "The way to solve X is through Y methodology." This positions your eventual pitch as the logical conclusion, not a sales ambush.

**Touch 4 — Present your specific solution** (email or landing page):
Now connect the framework to your product. "Here is how we implement that methodology." The prospect already agrees with the approach, so the product feels like an obvious fit.

**Touch 5 — Provide social proof and urgency** (email or outreach):
Customer stories, results, testimonials. Specificity matters — "Company X achieved Y in Z timeframe" beats "Trusted by thousands." Add legitimate urgency if available (cohort closing, price change, limited capacity).

**Touch 6 — Make the direct ask** (email or call):
Clear, specific CTA. "Ready to start?" or "Let's set up your account this week." By this point, the prospect has been warmed across multiple channels and the ask feels earned.

## Cross-Channel Coherence Rules

**Same positioning angle everywhere.** If your email campaign leads with speed, your social content must reinforce speed. If your landing page leads with simplicity, your outreach must lead with simplicity. Mixed positioning signals create confusion, and confused prospects do not buy.

**Voice adapts, message stays consistent.** LinkedIn tone is more professional. Twitter is more casual. Email can be conversational. But the core claim, the core proof points, and the core value proposition do not change across platforms.

**Cross-reference between channels.** Each channel should acknowledge the others exist. Email says "As we covered in our guide on X..." Social says "We just published a deep dive on Y — link in comments." This creates a sense of ecosystem, not isolated blasts.

**CTAs escalate across channels.** Early channels ask for low commitment (read, follow, subscribe). Middle channels ask for medium commitment (download, register, try). Late channels ask for high commitment (buy, upgrade, commit). Never ask for a high-commitment action on a low-commitment channel.

**Never duplicate messages across channels simultaneously.** If you send an email on Tuesday, do not post the same content on LinkedIn on Tuesday. The prospect who follows you on both channels sees redundancy, not reinforcement. Same core message, different angle or format, staggered timing.

## Campaign Timing Patterns

**Content-led cadence:**
Content piece publishes on Monday or Tuesday → social distribution across the same week (2-3 posts, different angles) → email to list within 48 hours of publish → paid amplification of top-performing social post by end of week.

**Outbound cadence:**
Cold email sends Tuesday-Thursday morning → LinkedIn connection request within 24 hours of email → content share (relevant article or resource) on day 3 → second email (new angle) on day 5 → breakup email on day 14.

**Launch cadence:**
PR and media outreach 2 weeks before launch → email teaser to existing list 1 week before → launch day: press coverage + email announcement + social blitz → paid ads begin 48 hours post-launch (after organic signals establish what messaging works) → follow-up nurture sequence for non-converters starting week 2.

**Timing gaps matter.** Too fast (multiple touches same day) feels aggressive. Too slow (weeks between touches) loses momentum. General rule: 2-3 days between touches in an active campaign, with no more than 2 touches on the same channel in a single week.
